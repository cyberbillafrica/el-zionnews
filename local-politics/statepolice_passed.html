SENATE PASSES STATE POLICE BILL, GRANTS GOVERNORS POWER TO APPOINT COMMISSIONERS

The Senate on Wednesday passed the State Police Service Establishment Bill 2026, marking a major milestone in Nigeria's ongoing efforts to reform its security architecture.

The landmark legislation, which is also a constitutional amendment bill, will bring an end to the country's 66-year-old centralised policing structure by establishing a dual policing system that allows state police services to operate alongside the Nigeria Police Force.

Senators voted on the 26-clause bill through a manual process, physically raising their hands to indicate support or opposition. The exercise ensured that lawmakers were individually counted, with those in support exceeding the constitutionally required two-thirds majority of 73 votes.

The voting method was adopted to prevent future disputes and ensure that every senator's position on the legislation was properly recorded.

One of the key concerns surrounding state police has been the possibility of political interference, particularly by state governors.

Addressing those fears, the Senate said the legislation contains several safeguards aimed at preventing abuse of power and protecting citizens' rights.

Bamidele stated: "One of the major concerns expressed regarding the establishment of State Police is the potential for political abuse by State Governments."

He added: "This Bill directly addresses that concern by prohibiting any governor from directing a State Police Service to target specific persons, political parties, groups or associations unlawfully."

"The Bill also expressly prohibits the use of police powers for partisan, ethnic, religious, sectional or personal purposes."

No senator opposed the bill during deliberations, underscoring the broad support it enjoyed within the Red Chamber.

The significance of the legislation was reflected in the presence of senior government officials and state governors who attended the proceedings.

A delegation from the Presidency led by the Chief of Staff to the President, Rt. Hon. Femi Gbajabiamila, was present in the Senate chamber to witness the debate and voting process.

Several governors were also in attendance, including the Chairman of the Southern Governors' Forum and Governor of Ogun State, Dapo Abiodun; Vice-Chairman of the Progressives Governors' Forum and Governor of Kaduna State, Uba Sani; Governor Lucky Aiyedatiwa of Ondo State; and Governor Biodun Oyebanji of Ekiti State.

Also present were the Attorney-General of the Federation and Minister of Justice, Lateef Fagbemi (SAN), as well as several state Attorneys-General.

However, the proceedings were not without controversy.

A mild drama unfolded when senators initially rejected a motion seeking to allow governors into the Senate chamber to witness the proceedings.

The motion, moved by Senate Leader Opeyemi Bamidele, was defeated, forcing the governors to observe proceedings from the gallery.

It was not immediately clear what informed the lawmakers' decision. However, sources indicated that some senators were still aggrieved over losing their re-election tickets and reportedly felt they had been "manipulated" out of the 2027 race by certain governors.

Concerned by the development, Senate President Godswill Akpabio appealed to lawmakers to reconsider their position.

Akpabio reminded senators that the constitutional amendment process would ultimately require the approval of state legislatures and cautioned against actions that could create unnecessary friction with governors.

He warned that governors could influence the decisions of their respective State Houses of Assembly if they felt they had been treated without due respect by the Senate.

Following interventions by Akpabio and Bamidele, senators eventually rescinded their earlier decision and permitted the governors to enter the chamber to witness the proceedings.

In a related development, the Senate also passed the Police Trust Fund Bill as part of wider efforts to strengthen Nigeria's security framework.

The legislation is designed to provide alternative funding mechanisms for the police, improve operational capacity and support efforts aimed at combating crime and insecurity across the country.

Lawmakers said the passage of both the State Police Service Establishment Bill and the Police Trust Fund Bill demonstrates the National Assembly's commitment to addressing the country's evolving security challenges through comprehensive legislative reforms.

With Senate approval secured, the State Police Bill will proceed through the remaining constitutional amendment processes, including consideration by State Houses of Assembly, before it can receive final approval and presidential assent.

If eventually enacted into law, the legislation is expected to fundamentally reshape Nigeria's policing structure and redefine the relationship between the federal and state governments in matters of law enforcement and public safety.

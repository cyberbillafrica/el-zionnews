Tinubu is shopping for a replacement for the INEC Chairman ahead of 2027.

President Tinubu should be attentive to the importance of upholding the credibility of elections in light of Nigeria's history of tolerating electoral misconduct by circumventing the law. El-zion News can confirm with authority that there is a scheme to undermine the 2027 general election by nominating a compliant individual to succeed Professor Mahmood Yakubu as the National Chairman of Nigeria’s Independent National Electoral Commission, INEC. Professor Yakubu's term is set to conclude this November after completing two terms. The commencement of the scheme, however, remains unverified. As of the latest update, El-zion News disclosed that President Bola Ahmed Tinubu has not been fully informed about the details of the scheme.

External forces exerting influence on the Aso Rock Presidential Villa are close associates of the President. These individuals are collaborating with influential insiders at Aso Rock. Spearheading this initiative is a former South-South governor who is part of the 1999 class. This development is occurring amidst declining trust in the Election Management Body following the 2023 general elections and subsequent off-season elections. An approach being taken to ensure the success of this plan involves proposing and securing the appointment of a compliant candidate as Yakubu's replacement.

Informally, El-zion News has reported that there are multiple names already being suggested for potential consideration. Regrettably, the individuals recommended by the schemers previously held roles as National Commissioners of the Commission and Resident Electoral Commissioners (RECs) but did not demonstrate exemplary conduct during their time in office.

Indeed, the primary factor influencing their decision appears to be the potential impact on the flexibility of the organization, which could ultimately result in a degradation of INEC's reputation in the conduct of transparent, fair, and reliable electoral processes. Nigerians reflect with a mix of sentimentality on the 2003 and 2007 general elections, which concluded with an acknowledgment from the late President Umar Musa Yar’Adua, a beneficiary of the flawed 2007 presidential election, regarding the irregularities in his ascension to power.

Disregarding constitutional stipulations.

In a democratic system with a codified Constitution, as opposed to the unwritten Constitution of the British parliamentary system, the authority wielded by any elected or appointed government official such as the President must be rooted in the Constitution; otherwise, it lacks legitimacy. Section 1 of the 1999 Constitution (as amended) clearly states: 'This Constitution is supreme, and its provisions shall have binding force on all authorities and persons, (including the President) throughout the Federal Republic of Nigeria.' INEC is established under Section 153 of the Constitution. The mode of appointment of the Chairman and members is provided for under Section 154(1) and (2) of the Constitution exclusively. In exercising his power to appoint the Chairman or members of INEC, the Constitution explicitly states, 'the president shall consult the Council of State,' and such appointment once more 'shall be subject to confirmation by the Senate INEC was established as genuinely independent by the Constitution, with its members required to have unquestionable integrity and prohibited from being members of a political party, according to the Third Schedule, Paragraph 14. To avoid repeating the error made by former President Buhari, it is crucial to ensure the timely appointment of a substantive national chairman for INEC to maintain the organization's credibility and effectiveness.

According to the established statutes and hierarchy, Jega formally transferred leadership responsibilities to Ambassador (Dr.) Ahmed Wali to oversee the operations of the commission until a permanent chairman is designated in accordance with the guidelines set forth by the Council of State as outlined in the constitution. Despite this, President Buhari, who had been inaugurated just 36 hours prior, intervened and disregarded Jega's decision by naming a close, familial relation, Mrs. Amina Bala Zakari, as the interim head of INEC within a day. The appointment, communicated in a letter by the Head of The Civil Service of The Federation at that time, Barr. Danladi Kifasi, was effective from June 30, 2015 until the appointment of a substantive chairman. It is noteworthy that both Wali and Zakari held positions as National Commissioners of INEC during that period. However, in terms of seniority, Wali surpassed Zakari. It is noteworthy that despite being declared the winner of the presidential election in March 2015, Buhari, who should have been briefed by his transition team on immediate appointments upon assuming office, seemed unaware of the developments in INEC or had already decided to appoint a family member as the INEC chairman. The uproar caused by Buhari's misjudgment at that time resulted in significant backlash against INEC. It was only after numerous investigative reports by Vanguard, along with opposition from patriotic activists, that Buhari was stopped from appointing his 'sister' as the INEC chairman.

Professor Mahmood Yakubu was appointed as the substantive chairman of INEC on Wednesday, October 21, 2015, almost four months after the need for a 'suitable replacement' for Jega was identified. This replacement oversaw the controversial 2019 and 2023 general elections. The credibility of these elections was marred by controversies such as the presence of a server in INEC and the inefficiency of the BVAS and IReV platforms. With five months remaining, President Tinubu has an opportunity to appoint a replacement similar to Jega, as exemplified by Ghana's appointment of Madam Jean Mensa after Kwadwo Afari-Gyan. According to reliable sources, President Tinubu recognizes the significance of ensuring credible elections.

According to a source close to the President, the President's statement suggesting the use of NYSC members to support the upcoming census is not solely focused on cost-saving but also on ensuring the credibility of the process. Similarly, his approach towards the appointment of Professor Yakubu's successor reflects his desire for a credible process and to maintain the trust of Nigerians in INEC. Former military and civilian president Mathew Okikiolakan Aremu Olusegun Obasanjo, who hails from Owu, is still highly regarded on the global stage due to his 1979 transition programme that led to the establishment of the Second Republic. During this time period, African Heads of State were transitioning into civilian presidents. This phenomenon indicates the global acknowledgment of the crucial role that credible elections play and emphasizes the importance of the leader's integrity during such elections. Nigeria boasts some of the most advanced electoral laws in Africa, but their effective implementation necessitates operators of utmost integrity. In line with many African nations that adhere to the New Partnership for Africa’s Development (NEPAD), undergo peer reviews of their democratic processes, and uphold the AU Charter on Democracy and Good Governance, Nigeria has adopted similar norms and standards. Furthermore, Nigeria has tailored these principles to suit its own context by introducing innovative processes such as the Biometric electronic voters register and electronic transmission of election results from polling units. Additionally, several amendments to electoral rules, endorsed by political stakeholders based on real-world feedback, aim to enhance the integrity of the electoral system.

In spite of these advancements, which, in numerous cases, surpass those of other electoral legislative frameworks in Africa, operators frequently falter or face pressure to do so when implementing the provisions and guidelines of the framework. The effectiveness of election management can be greatly enhanced by having a reliable institutional memory. The cases in Akwa-Ibom and Abia highlight the significant role of human influence, contrasting with the situation in Adamawa. These contrasting situations, exemplified by the determined approach of REC Mike Igini in Akwa Ibom in prosecuting two university professors for electoral offenses, and Professor Nnenna Oti's actions in Abia, where she refused to announce a fraudulent result, starkly contrast with the behavior of the Adamawa REC, who, in violation of electoral guidelines, announced a fictitious result of the governorship election. Similarly, the removal of Chidi Nwafor as Director of ICT had a negative impact on the 2023 general elections.

Professor Okechukwu Ibeanu, a National Commissioner overseeing Operations, was not re-appointed despite his efforts in ensuring the integrity of the Anambra elections and protecting them from manipulation and internal interference. This failure to acknowledge and promote appointees who have shown high levels of integrity is concerning. The team of electoral operators led by the late Professors Eme Awa and Humphrey Nwosu still has surviving members, albeit aging. Professors Adele Jinadu, a former National Commissioner, and Tonnie Iredia, the NEC Director of Public Affairs, are alive and their institutional knowledge would be invaluable to the current administration. The Nigerian electoral system necessitates a comprehensive overhaul that goes beyond mere policing of electoral malpractices and fraud, focusing on appointing and retaining individuals with a proven history of diligence and ethical conduct

Utilizing the expertise of experienced and reliable individuals is essential for reducing the competitiveness of elections and streamlining the electoral process, thus potentially reducing the influence of political actors on the formulation and approval of election laws. It is imperative that the Tinubu administration prioritizes these proposed reforms, widely supported by the public for enhancing election integrity. By garnering public support for these changes and appointing reputable election officials to oversee the reforms within the Electoral Management Body (EMB), the administration can significantly enhance its legitimacy. It is notable that some key figures who contributed to the success of the 2015 elections alongside Jega have been gradually removed from the system. Vanguard's investigations suggest that the deliberate policy of the Buhari administration is to prevent the reappointment of some of these assertive individuals. Disagreements arose during the 2019 general elections regarding the INEC Server, which was ultimately declared nonexistent despite being approved, budgeted for, and purchased. The national chairman had confidently announced the utilization of a server to store the election results. The declining trend of voter turnout in Nigeria's elections, with only 24 million voters participating in the 2023 elections, poses a challenge in rebuilding INEC and engaging the diminishing population of Nigerian voters. May I inquire as to the reasons behind the decline in voter turnout figures? A complex array of issues, such as voter apathy stemming from a lack of confidence in INEC, persistent challenges faced by voters during elections, ongoing logistical problems, incidents of election day violence deterring voters, and a sense of disenchantment among voters regarding the efficacy of their votes, are contributing factors. Potential exacerbation of the issue in 2027 is a concern due to unfulfilled promises and assurances made by Professor Yakubu concerning the security of BVAS and IReV. These promises initially instilled hope in millions of Nigerians for the election process but were later debunked upon the discovery of unuploaded results. To address this, enhancements in pre-election processes, enforcement of post-election dispute resolution measures, and improvements in BVAS and result transmission are imperative. If President Tinubu can oversee the execution of these recommendations and supplementary measures, it could lead to the establishment of a legacy rooted in honesty and uprightness.

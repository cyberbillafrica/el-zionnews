HOW IS HEPATITIS A MANAGED?

EL-ZION NEWS   

Hepatitis A is a short-term liver infection caused by the hepatitis A virus. Common symptoms include diarrhea, dark-colored urine, fatigue, fever, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and jaundice—yellowing of the eyes and skin.

If you suspect exposure to the virus, you may be eligible for preventive treatment. However, once the infection sets in, there is no specific cure. “It usually resolves on its own with time,” and treatment focuses on symptom relief as the body naturally clears the virus.

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

Post-exposure prophylaxis can prevent hepatitis A if administered within two weeks of exposure. Potential exposure may occur through:

  • Eating contaminated food from an affected restaurant
  • Consuming food involved in a hepatitis A-related recall
  • Close contact with someone infected
  • Traveling to regions with high infection rates

PEP is appropriate if you haven't been vaccinated or previously infected. For those older than one year, it typically involves a hepatitis A vaccine. Individuals over 40 may also receive immune globulin, a concentrated antibody treatment.

Side effects of PEP may include mild soreness at the injection site, headache, fatigue, and appetite loss, but these effects are usually temporary.

Supportive Care

Since the body usually clears hepatitis A on its own, home care focuses on easing symptoms and boosting the immune system. Recommendations include:

Rest

Fatigue from the infection can make normal activities difficult. Rest supports recovery and strengthens immunity. If you're working, consider asking for lighter duties, more breaks, or a flexible schedule.

Hydration

Symptoms like vomiting and diarrhea can cause fluid loss. Staying hydrated is crucial. Water, bone broth, and sugar-free sports drinks can help replace lost fluids and electrolytes.

Balanced Diet

Eating nutrient-rich foods such as fruits, vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains can promote liver recovery. Avoiding high-fat foods may help reduce nausea and vomiting.

Other Lifestyle Adjustments

Doctors often advise avoiding alcohol and tobacco during recovery, as both can worsen liver inflammation.

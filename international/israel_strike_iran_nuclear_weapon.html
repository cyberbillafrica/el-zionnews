ISRAELI STRIKES TARGETED IRAN'S NUCLEAR FACILITIES — BUT WHAT WAS THE EXTENT OF THE DAMAGE?

EL-ZION NEWS   

Israel's extraordinary airstrikes on Iran were aimed at a high-stakes objective: dismantling the nation's controversial nuclear program.

The Israeli military focused on three major nuclear sites—Natanz, Isfahan, and Fordow—as well as several leading scientists involved in nuclear research and development.

Initial assessments based on satellite images and expert evaluations suggest that the attacks caused considerable damage at Natanz and Isfahan. However, the full extent remains uncertain, particularly since many of Iran’s most sensitive nuclear assets are hidden deep underground.

An Israeli military official claimed during a briefing that the operations at Natanz and Isfahan caused “significant” damage. Iran, on the other hand, insisted that any destruction to its nuclear infrastructure was minor.

“The whole (nuclear) supply chain has been disrupted,” said Ali Vaez, Iran Project Director at the International Crisis Group. He explained the importance of the targeted facilities, which are all integral parts of the multi-step process for creating a nuclear weapon.

“This chain is now broken, but it could be put back together in a matter of months, because Iran has both the knowhow and the material that is required for it,” he warned. “This is not a problem that will go away simply with aerial strikes.”

Natanz

Preliminary reports indicate that Israel’s assault on the Natanz nuclear site was highly effective. Two U.S. officials noted that the strikes went beyond damaging above-ground buildings—they disrupted power systems in the underground areas where uranium enrichment centrifuges are stored.

“This was a full-spectrum blitz,” another source familiar with the intelligence stated.

The airstrikes destroyed the surface-level Pilot Fuel Enrichment Plant, operational since 2003 and used for enriching uranium up to 60% purity. (Weapons-grade uranium requires 90% enrichment.)

Satellite photos captured before and after the incident show major damage to at least two structures, though their exact functions remain unclear.

The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) confirmed that Natanz's electrical systems—including primary and backup generators—were destroyed. This aligns with accounts from U.S. officials, who indicated the lower levels lost power where centrifuges are housed.

While the fortified underground sections were not directly hit, the IAEA noted that the blackout “may have damaged the centrifuges there.”

Centrifuges operate by rapidly spinning uranium gas. “These are machines that spin at the speed of light, and if they are suddenly turned off, some of them might explode or suffer unrecoverable damage,” Vaez explained.

According to the Nuclear Threat Initiative (NTI), Natanz includes six buildings above ground and three below—two capable of housing 50,000 centrifuges.

Despite the attack, external radiation levels remained normal. “The level of radioactivity outside the Natanz site has remained unchanged and at normal levels,” the IAEA stated. “However, due to the impacts, there is radiological and chemical contamination inside the facilities in Natanz,” though they described it as manageable.

“There’s also a stockpile of highly enriched uranium in Natanz, in one of the buildings that Israel could strike, but hasn’t,” Vaez said. “The risk there is an explosion could basically turn that stockpile into a dirty nuclear bomb that would create radioactive contamination with disastrous environmental and humanitarian effects.”

Isfahan

Damage to the Isfahan nuclear center in central Iran was harder to assess in the immediate aftermath. Conflicting narratives emerged from both sides.

The IAEA later confirmed that four critical buildings at the site had sustained damage.

That contradicted earlier remarks from Behrouz Kamalvandi, spokesperson for Iran’s Atomic Energy Organization, who said the damage was limited and that only a shed had caught fire.

Israel’s version differed. A military official said during a briefing that the site experienced substantial damage.

Satellite images taken Saturday revealed visible damage to three buildings in the complex, though the fourth referenced by the IAEA wasn't easily identifiable.

Kamalvandi claimed some equipment had been relocated from Natanz and Isfahan before the attacks—though this remains unverified.

According to NTI, the Isfahan site—built with Chinese assistance in 1984—employs 3,000 scientists and is thought to be central to Iran’s nuclear program. It “operates three small Chinese-supplied research reactors,” and includes a conversion plant, fuel production facilities, and labs.

During a briefing, an Israeli defense official claimed to have “concrete intelligence” that Iran was “moving forward to a nuclear bomb” at Isfahan. Iran, meanwhile, has continued to assert that its nuclear activities are peaceful and not geared toward weaponization.

Fordow

The Fordow Fuel Enrichment Plant, hidden in the mountains near Qom, is a significantly harder target.

Although it was among the sites Israel targeted, the IAEA reported no damage, and the Israeli military made no claims to the contrary. Iranian media said an Israeli drone was shot down near the site.

“The expectation has always been that Israel would not be able to reach (Fordow), because it would need the kind of bunker-buster, massive ordinance bombs that only the United States has,” said Vaez.

Fordow could prove decisive in whether Israel’s actions truly curbed Iran’s nuclear capabilities.

In 2023, the IAEA found uranium enriched to 83.7% purity at Fordow—just short of weapons-grade.

“If Fordow remains operational, Israel’s attacks may barely slow Iran’s path to the bomb,” wrote James M. Acton of the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. He added that while Israel might block access to the site, destroying it outright would be highly difficult.

Other Targets

The Arak nuclear complex, housing a heavy-water reactor that could produce plutonium, appears to have escaped damage.

While nuclear facilities were the main focus, Israel also struck military and Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) sites.

In Piranshahr, near the Iraqi border, satellite images show a small military structure destroyed. In western Tehran, a large IRGC facility was significantly damaged, with its roof blown off.

One of the top IRGC commanders, Maj. Gen. Hossein Salami, was reportedly killed in the Israeli strikes on Friday.

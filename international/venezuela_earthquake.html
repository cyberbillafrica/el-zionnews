TWIN EARTHQUAKES ROCK VENEZUELA'S CAPITAL, RAISING FEARS OF MASS CASUALTIES

Venezuela has declared a state of emergency after two powerful earthquakes struck the country, causing widespread destruction, collapsing dozens of buildings and leaving at least 32 people dead, with experts warning that the casualty figure could rise significantly in the coming days.

Interim leader Delcy Rodríguez announced the emergency measures following the disaster, revealing that hundreds of people had sustained injuries while search and rescue operations continued across affected areas.

The two earthquakes struck within less than a minute of each other, with the first measuring 7.2 in magnitude and the second reaching 7.5. According to the United States Geological Survey (USGS), the tremors represent the most powerful seismic event to hit Venezuela since 1900.

Addressing the nation in a televised broadcast on Wednesday night, Rodríguez urged citizens to remain vigilant and avoid damaged structures.

"We ask the population to remain calm and united."

The earthquakes, among the strongest ever recorded in Venezuela, were felt across large parts of the country. However, the most severe destruction was reported in and around the capital, Caracas.

Videos circulating on social media showed scenes of panic at Maiquetía International Airport, where passengers rushed through terminals seeking safety as debris fell from damaged structures.

Rodríguez confirmed that the airport had been shut down after sustaining extensive damage and announced the suspension of metro and rail services as authorities assessed the impact of the disaster.

She added: "We send our immediate condolences to those who have lost relatives."

The acting president did not provide an updated casualty figure during the address but acknowledged that the number of victims was expected to increase as emergency teams continued rescue efforts.

According to the USGS, the seismic event consisted of a magnitude 7.2 foreshock followed 39 seconds later by a stronger magnitude 7.5 mainshock.

The agency said the epicentre was located west of the coastal community of Morón, approximately 168 kilometres west of Caracas, at a depth of about 13 kilometres.

Warning of the scale of the disaster, the USGS stated: "High casualties and extensive damage are probable and the disaster is likely widespread."

Reports from affected communities indicate that multiple buildings collapsed across parts of Caracas and surrounding districts, trapping residents beneath rubble and prompting large-scale rescue operations.

Providing further details on the disaster, Venezuela's Interior Minister, Diosdado Cabello, described the situation as extremely serious, warning that several areas had suffered significant structural damage.

Speaking to state broadcaster VTV, Cabello said: "A seismic event that everything suggests was considerably above 7 points has taken place. There are several complicated areas … very alarming areas from the visual point of view, with buildings and homes that have collapsed."

The minister also cautioned residents about the possibility of additional seismic activity in the hours and days ahead.

"Normally this kind of event is followed by aftershocks, which could also bring down some structures that were damaged by the main event," Cabello warned.

Reports from Caracas indicate that some of the worst destruction occurred in Altamira, an upscale district that hosts several foreign embassies.

A Guardian correspondent at the scene observed at least three buildings that had collapsed after the earthquakes struck shortly after 6 p.m. on Wednesday.

Emergency responders were deployed to multiple locations as reports emerged that several people were trapped beneath the rubble. Authorities had yet to confirm the exact number of victims or fatalities in the affected areas.

Outside one collapsed building, distraught relatives gathered as rescue teams searched for survivors. Witnesses reported seeing a man weeping and repeatedly calling out the name of his grandmother, whom he feared was trapped inside the debris.

Residents who experienced the earthquake firsthand described scenes of panic and devastation.

Olky Barrero, a 56-year-old teacher who joined rescue efforts near a collapsed six-storey building, said: "It was horrible."

She added: "We hope to God that there are as few victims as possible. We're praying."

Recalling the moment the earthquake struck, Barrero said: "Where I was, it felt like the walls were going to fall on top of us, they were shaking back and forth, this way and that."

Her comments came as rescue workers pulled at least two victims from the wreckage, including one person who was reportedly still alive.

Another resident, José Morillo, said several members of his family were believed to be trapped inside one of the collapsed structures.

"My brother, my son and nephews are all inside. I have faith. I believe in God a lot. I hope everyone is OK – but uncertainty is torture," the 61-year-old said as emergency personnel continued search operations.

Across the capital, thick clouds of dust rose above damaged neighbourhoods while images circulating online showed residents fleeing unsafe buildings with their belongings and pets.

One of the hardest-hit locations appeared to be La Guaira, a major port city north of Caracas on Venezuela's Caribbean coast.

La Guaira is home to the Simón Bolívar International Airport, the country's main international gateway, and several densely populated hillside communities.

Authorities reported extensive damage across parts of the city, including the near-total destruction of the beachfront Eduard's Hotel Boutique.

In nearby Catia La Mar, Venezuela's naval academy and several high-rise residential buildings were also said to have suffered severe structural damage.

The scale of the destruction was further highlighted by eyewitness footage from San Bernardino, a district in northern Caracas where another building collapsed.

In one widely shared video, a resident could be heard saying: "There are many injured people inside. It's a disaster."

Elsewhere in Baruta, a suburb of Caracas, civil defence personnel were seen using stretchers to evacuate victims from damaged buildings following a landslide reportedly triggered by the earthquake.

Emergency responders worked through the debris as rescue efforts intensified across affected communities.

Baruta Mayor Darwin González shared footage on social media showing rescue workers pulling a woman from the rubble of a collapsed structure.

Appealing for calm, González said: "We call on people to remain calm and civil at this time."

Messages of support also poured in from prominent Venezuelan figures, including exiled opposition leader and Nobel Peace Prize laureate María Corina Machado.

In a post on X, Machado expressed solidarity with those affected by the disaster, writing: "My heart, my infinite embrace, and my prayers are with every Venezuelan home in these hours of anguish. May strength, serenity and solidarity prevail among us in the face of this difficult moment."

As rescue operations intensified, the United States Embassy in Caracas issued an advisory urging American citizens to avoid damaged areas, stay away from compromised buildings and seek secure shelter.

International concern has also grown following the disaster, with several countries expressing readiness to assist Venezuela as emergency responders continue search and recovery efforts.

United States President Donald Trump offered support to the South American nation, describing the earthquakes as devastating.

Trump said: "The two major earthquakes that just hit the great people of Venezuela are both massive in scale and have left a devastating number of deaths."

The US government also announced plans to provide emergency assistance to support rescue and humanitarian operations in the affected areas.

In a statement released early Thursday, US Secretary of State Marco Rubio said the United States was mobilising resources to assist those impacted by the disaster.

Rubio stated: "Immediately deploying search and rescue teams, medical resources, and humanitarian assistance to Venezuela."

Rescue workers continue to search through the rubble across several affected communities as authorities assess the full extent of the disaster and work to account for those still missing.

With aftershocks still possible and emergency operations ongoing, officials fear the death toll could rise substantially as more information emerges from some of the worst-hit regions of the country.

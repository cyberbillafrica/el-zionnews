WHEN IRAN'S SUPREME LEADER RESURFACES, HE WILL FACE A TRANSFORMED NATION

EL-ZION NEWS   

After spending nearly two weeks in a hidden bunker somewhere in Iran during the country’s war with Israel, the supreme leader, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, 86, may now take the opportunity provided by a temporary ceasefire to reemerge.

He is believed to have remained completely out of communication, fearing assassination by Israel. Reports suggest that even senior government officials had no direct contact with him.

Despite the ceasefire, brokered by U.S. President Donald Trump and the Emir of Qatar, caution is still warranted. Although Trump is said to have urged Israel not to target Iran’s supreme leader, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu did not rule it out.

Should Khamenei emerge from hiding, he will encounter a nation deeply scarred. He is expected to appear on state television to declare victory, and he will attempt to rebuild his image—but he will also face a transformed landscape and new realities.

The war has left Iran weakened and has diminished Khamenei’s stature.

Murmurs of dissent at the top

During the conflict, Israel swiftly took control of large parts of Iranian airspace and struck key military infrastructure. Several top commanders of the Revolutionary Guard and the army were eliminated.

Though the full scope of military damage remains disputed, the repeated attacks on military and Revolutionary Guard bases suggest that Iran's military capacity has been severely degraded. Militarization had consumed vast national resources for years.

Iran’s known nuclear sites—developed at the cost of international sanctions and hundreds of billions of dollars—have been bombed, though the full extent of the damage is still uncertain.

This has prompted many Iranians to question the entire strategy and its outcomes.

A significant portion of the population now holds Ayatollah Khamenei, who has led since 1989, responsible for steering the nation into a devastating conflict with Israel and the United States.

He is blamed for pursuing an ideological mission to destroy Israel—an aim many Iranians do not support—and for the belief that possessing nuclear weapons would secure the regime's future.

Sanctions have crushed the Iranian economy, turning a former top oil exporter into a struggling nation.

“It is difficult to estimate how much longer the Iranian regime can survive under such significant strain, but this looks like the beginning of the end,” says Professor Lina Khatib, a visiting scholar at Harvard University.

“Ali Khamenei is likely to become the Islamic Republic's last 'Supreme Leader' in the full sense of the word.”

During the height of the war, a semi-official Iranian news agency reported that some senior regime veterans had called on the quietist religious scholars in Qom—who operate independently of Khamenei—to intervene and instigate leadership change.

“There will be a reckoning,” said Professor Ali Ansari, founding director of the Institute of Iranian Studies at the University of St Andrews.

“It's quite clear that there are huge disagreements within the leadership, and there's also huge unhappiness among ordinary people.”

'Anger and frustration will take root'

Over the past two weeks, many Iranians have grappled with conflicting feelings—the urge to defend their country versus resentment toward the regime. Citizens came together, not in support of the leadership, but to support one another.

In towns and villages, residents opened their homes to those fleeing bombardments, shopkeepers lowered prices for basic necessities, and neighbors checked on each other’s wellbeing.

Yet many are also aware that Israel may be seeking regime change—a goal that many inside Iran share, albeit with caution about foreign-imposed solutions.

“If the regime is unable to supply basic goods and services, then there will be growing anger and frustration,” adds Prof. Ansari.

“I see it as a staged process. I don't see it as something that, necessarily, in a popular sense, will take root until long after the bombing is over.”

Few believe the ceasefire announced Monday will hold—and many suspect Israel has not yet finished its campaign, given its current dominance in the airspace over Iran.

Iran’s ballistic missile silos

One major Iranian capability that has largely survived is its network of ballistic missile silos, hidden in underground mountain tunnels that proved difficult for Israel to target.

Israeli Defense Forces Chief of Staff Eyal Zamir stated that Israel’s opening assault took place with the knowledge that Iran possessed around 2,500 surface-to-surface missiles. Some of these missiles caused severe damage and loss of life in Israel.

Israeli authorities remain concerned that up to 1,500 missiles could still be in Iranian hands.

There are also fears in Tel Aviv, Washington, and other global capitals that Iran may accelerate efforts to develop a nuclear bomb—an ambition Tehran has consistently denied.

Though Iran’s nuclear facilities appear to have been set back or rendered inoperative during the bombardments, Iranian officials claim their highly enriched uranium stockpile has been moved to a secret, secure location.

This stockpile, enriched to 60%, could be further refined to weapons-grade 90%, and is reportedly enough to produce nine nuclear bombs. Prior to the war, Iran announced the completion of another secret enrichment facility.

The Iranian parliament has voted to sharply reduce cooperation with the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA). If approved, this could position Iran on the brink of leaving the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT), as hardliners rally for a nuclear breakout.

While Ayatollah Khamenei may believe his regime has narrowly survived, at 86 and in declining health, he is also likely thinking about how to secure its continuity—possibly through the appointment of another senior cleric or a leadership council.

Meanwhile, surviving senior Revolutionary Guard commanders may seek to maintain control and influence from behind the scenes.
